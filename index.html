Sinking Nations

Rising seas are often described as a global trend, but the danger is not spread evenly. Low-lying islands and coastal nations face very different levels of exposure depending on their land, population, income, and geography.

Hook

Sea level rise is measured in inches and centimeters, but for some nations it can mean losing homes, roads, farms, and cultural sites.

Problem

Global averages hide the fact that small islands and low-lying coastal nations can face much greater danger than larger inland countries.

Takeaway

The countries most exposed to rising seas are often not the countries most responsible for historical climate pollution.

How this becomes an explanation

The project should compare risk, capacity, and responsibility

Who is physically exposed?

Use sea-level scenarios to show which countries lose the largest share of land or have the most people in low-elevation coastal zones.

Who has less room to adapt?

Bring in land area, population density, and economic indicators so vulnerability is about more than coastline length.

Who contributed least?

Compare exposure with emissions contribution to show the climate justice pattern in your writeup: high risk does not always match high responsibility.

Progress and challenges

What have we done so far?

So far, we have built Sinking Nations around an interactive country map. Users can move a sea-level rise slider from 0 to 2 meters, hover over vulnerable nations, and see estimated exposed land, impacted area, and exposed population update in the story card. This keeps the project focused on the geographic and human stakes of sea-level rise.

What will be most challenging to design, and why?

The most challenging part will be combining datasets that measure different parts of the problem. Sea-level exposure, population, land area, country income, and emissions data may come from different sources and may not use the same country names or years. We will need to clean and join those datasets carefully before the final visualization can be trustworthy. Another challenge will be avoiding a map that only looks interesting but does not explain the story clearly. The final design needs annotations and comparisons that help readers understand why some nations face much higher risks despite contributing less to climate change. We also need to choose metrics carefully so the project does not oversimplify vulnerability into only one number.

Data plan and next steps

The final project should combine sea-level rise or coastal exposure data with country-level indicators such as population, land area, region, GDP or income group, and emissions contribution. Potential sources include World Bank indicators, Our World in Data emissions data, and coastal exposure or elevation datasets.

Next step: replace the sample exposure curves with cleaned real country-level coastal exposure data so the slider estimates are transparent and defensible.
